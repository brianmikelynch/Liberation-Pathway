Phase 1 · Somatic foundation

Regulate

Rescue & Resuscitate · Body & Safety

Reboot · Your nervous system

Step 1

Learn your own stress signals. Tight jaw, racing heart, shallow breath, or going blank. Get curious about what your body does first.

Step 2

Pick one consistent time to wake up each day. Even on hard days. Your nervous system responds to predictability.

Step 3

Practice one grounding technique daily. Try naming five things you can see, four you can touch, three you can hear. Stay with it for two minutes.

Step 4

Ask yourself: what does calm actually feel like in my body? Build a sensory memory of safety so you have something to return to.

Regenerate · Physical self-care

Step 1

Aim for one nourishing meal a day that you eat sitting down, without a screen. That's it. Start there.

Step 2

Find one form of movement that doesn't feel like punishment. Walking, stretching, dancing alone. It counts.

Step 3

Track water, sleep, and food as information, not performance. You're learning what your body needs, not grading yourself.

Step 4

Start noticing the difference between a physical craving and a physical need. They feel different. Practice telling them apart.

Recuperate · Rest without guilt

Step 1

Name the belief that makes rest feel wrong. "I haven't earned it." "I'll fall apart." "People will think I'm lazy." Just name it.

Step 2

Schedule one rest period this week that you protect like an appointment. Not optional, not earned. Just yours.

Step 3

Notice the difference between numbing (substances, scrolling to go numb) and actual rest (something that leaves you feeling more like yourself).

Step 4

Practice saying "my body needs this" without adding a justification. You don't owe an explanation for rest.

Reconstitute · Safe structure

Step 1

Pick one daily ritual that signals safety to your body — morning coffee, an evening walk, a consistent bedtime. Make it reliable.

Step 2

Remove one thing from your immediate environment that reliably pulls you toward a harmful pattern. Even a small change counts.

Step 3

Write a simple crisis plan. Who do I call? What do I do first? Where can I go? Have it somewhere you can find it fast.

Step 4

Ask yourself: what does "safe enough" look like for me right now? Not perfect. Not healed. Just safe enough for today.

Phase 2 · Relational

Relate

Reanimate · Connection & Self-Kindness

Reunite · With yourself

Step 1

Write down three things that were true about you before the wound. Who were you trying to become?

Step 2

Name one interest or belief you suppressed because it wasn't safe to show. Just naming it is a step.

Step 3

Once a week, do one thing purely because you enjoy it, not because it helps anyone else or proves anything.

Step 4

The next time you catch yourself becoming whoever the room needs, notice it. You don't have to change it yet. Just notice.

Realign · Your values

Step 1

Name three things you actually value, not what you think you should value. Honesty, creativity, safety, freedom. Your list is yours.

Step 2

Notice the next time a situation pushes against one of those values. You don't have to act yet. Just name it to yourself.

Step 3

Write one boundary you've never enforced and draft what it would actually sound like out loud. Practice saying it alone first.

Step 4

Learn the difference between guilt (I did something that hurt someone) and shame (I am something wrong). They are not the same feeling.

Reprogram · The inner critic

Step 1

Write down the five things your inner critic says most often. Get specific. "You're so stupid" or "You always ruin it."

Step 2

For each one, ask: whose voice is this really? When did you first hear someone say this to you or about you?

Step 3

Find a counter-statement that is honest, not fake-positive. Not "I'm amazing" but "That's one voice, not the whole truth."

Step 4

When you catch yourself using harsh self-talk, try replacing it with something you'd say to a friend in the same situation.

Reinforce · Safe connections

Step 1

Think of one person in your life who has been consistently safe, meaning they don't judge, dismiss, or use what you share against you.

Step 2

Share one small, low-stakes true thing with that person. See what happens. You're gathering evidence about safety.

Step 3

Start noticing when someone reaches out to you. A text, a joke, a check-in. Those are bids for connection. Notice them before you brush them off.

Step 4

After a genuinely good interaction, pause before minimizing it. Let it land for a moment. Your nervous system needs practice receiving good things.

Phase 3 · Cortical

Reason

Rebuild · Identity & Purpose

Reclaim · Who you really are

Step 1

Write down three things that felt true about you before the trauma or addiction took hold. They don't have to be big.

Step 2

Name one interest, value, or dream you pushed aside just to survive. Acknowledge that you had to do that.

Step 3

Once a week, keep one promise you made to yourself, no matter how small. Notice how that feels.

Step 4

The next time you automatically say yes to keep the peace, pause first. Ask yourself what you actually want.

Reframe · Your story

Step 1

Name the story your pain tells about you. Examples: "I ruin everything," "I'm too much," "I don't deserve good things."

Step 2

Look for even one small piece of evidence that contradicts that story. It doesn't have to be perfect evidence.

Step 3

Practice saying: "This happened to me, but it doesn't define what I am." Say it even when it doesn't feel true yet.

Step 4

Ask yourself what your experience has quietly taught you — things like empathy, pattern recognition, or the ability to read a room. Those are real skills.

Redeem · Your pain

Step 1

Think of one person who is earlier on the same path you've been on. You don't have to do anything yet. Just hold them in mind.

Step 2

Finish this sentence: "Because of what I've been through, I want to..." Let it be honest, not heroic.

Step 3

When you feel ready, find one small way to witness someone else's struggle without trying to fix it. Presence is enough.

Step 4

Consider what role you might play in your community as someone who has survived. You get to define what that looks like.

Renovate · Your life design

Step 1

Look honestly at your current relationships. Which ones reinforce the old version of you? Which ones make room for who you're becoming?

Step 2

Identify one structural change in your life that would better reflect your values now, whether that's a routine, a place, or a relationship.

Step 3

Write a simple 90-day becoming plan. Not a recovery plan. A growth plan. What do you want to be moving toward?

Step 4

Celebrate one real milestone with at least one other person. Let them witness it. Breaking silence is part of the healing.
